## Key Tactics

1. **The single biggest failure mode named directly: a weak first 20-40 seconds.** If the hook doesn't create curiosity immediately, the prospect bounces. Most people "waffle" instead of following a structure.
2. **3-part hook, in order:**
   - **Face** — show yourself in a visibly good mood (smile, a small wave, "Hey [name]") — the easy, low-effort part.
   - **Trigger/observation** — reference something specific and real you found on their profile/site/socials (a recent post, an "about us" line, a past job, a banner image) — takes ~5-10 seconds to find but is what makes it land as personal rather than templated.
   - **Light humor tied to the observation** — makes you read as a real person, not an automated script. Creator's example: noticing a prospect used to be a physiotherapist, joking about his own bad back from sitting all day, before pivoting to the real reason for reaching out.
3. **The "quick fix" — show a small, real, immediately-visible improvement idea before pitching anything.** Not a criticism, an actual mini-demo: point at something concrete on their screen and show what a better version would look like, tied directly to the service being offered. This is the payload that makes the curiosity from the hook pay off — the video has to deliver on what the hook promised within the first minute.
4. **Structure after the quick fix — "lead-in" into a Problem/Mistake/Solution/Outcome sequence:**
   - Name your ICP's single most common recurring pain point in one sentence.
   - List 2-3 "mistakes" — what prospects typically do *before* working with someone like you (their default, ineffective approach).
   - Tie each mistake to your specific solution.
   - Tie each solution to a concrete outcome (time saved, money saved, consistency gained) — not vague benefits.
5. **Social proof placement: right after the pitch, brief, and matched to the specific prospect's category.** One testimonial/case study/screenshot from someone as close as possible to this exact prospect's situation — not a generic proof point.
6. **CTA must be as low-friction as physically possible.** The stated failure mode: asking for a deep commitment (a call, an in-depth reply) instead of a trivial yes/no action. Three concrete low-friction CTA options given:
   - "Click this button to book a call" (native Loom CTA button — one click, not a page navigation)
   - "Just reply and I'll send you [specific small deliverable]" (e.g. a free audit)
   - "React to this video/message" (e.g. Loom's native thumbs-up reaction, which notifies the sender — creates a re-engagement trigger without requiring the prospect to write anything)
7. **Screen-share backdrop is flexible — not limited to "their website."** Creator explicitly uses LinkedIn profile, Instagram profile, Facebook profile, or website interchangeably as the visual backdrop, chosen based on wherever the prospect is most active/has the most personalization material. The specific choice of backdrop is itself part of what triggers curiosity ("why is this guy on my LinkedIn profile?").
8. **Channel trust differential, explicitly stated:** cold email links get clicked less than the same link sent via LinkedIn DM, because on LinkedIn/social platforms the recipient can immediately verify the sender is a real person by checking their profile — that verification path doesn't exist in email. Creator states this trust effect also applies to Instagram and Facebook.
9. **Stated benchmark:** roughly 1 booked meeting per 3 personalized Loom outreach attempts, each video taking 3-5 minutes to record and personalize.

## Frameworks

### Full Cold Loom Script Structure
```
[0:00–0:10] Face — smile, wave, greet by name
[0:10–0:30] Trigger/observation — something specific and real about them + light humor
[0:30–0:40] Transition line — "I've got a quick idea that could help you achieve [outcome]"
[0:40–1:00] Quick fix — show a real, small, concrete improvement idea live on screen
[1:00–1:15] Lead-in — "That's not actually why I made this video — I work with [ICP] helping them [outcome], and thought I'd show you how"
[1:15–1:45] Social proof — one relevant example/testimonial/case study, brief
[1:45–2:15] Pain naming — one sentence naming the ICP's most common recurring frustration
[2:15–2:45] Mistake → Solution → Outcome (2-3 mistakes, each tied to a fix and a concrete result)
[2:45–3:00] Low-friction CTA — native button, "reply and I'll send X," or a reaction/emoji ask
```

### Low-Friction CTA Options (in order of friction, lowest first)
```
1. Native platform reaction (thumbs-up/emoji react) — zero typing required
2. "Just reply [yes/interested] and I'll send you [small deliverable]" — one word reply
3. Native CTA button linking directly to a booking page — one click, no typing
(Avoid: "let's hop on a call to discuss" — too high-commitment as a first ask)
```

## Exact Phrases Worth Testing

> "Hey [name], noticed [specific real observation]. Don't worry, I'm not stalking your profile."
> Context: hook opener pairing observation with self-aware humor to defuse the "how did you find this" discomfort.

> "That's not actually why I was making this video — I work with people in [X] helping them [Y], and thought I'd explain how you could do the same."
> Context: the pivot line from the "quick fix" demo into the actual pitch — reframes the free value just shown as a preview of the real offer, not the offer itself.

> "Most [ICP] struggle to [core pain], which results in [concrete downstream cost]."
> Context: one-sentence pain-naming template — forces specificity (a cost, not just a feeling).

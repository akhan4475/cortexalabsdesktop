## Key Tactics

1. **Personalization ≠ using their first name** — Using a first name while having zero understanding of the business is worse than not personalizing at all. It exposes that you're running a templated system and the prospect immediately sees through it.
2. **Permission + authenticity must coexist** — Asking permission is a sales technique. It only works when paired with genuine rapport and understanding. Asking permission from someone you clearly know nothing about feels robotic and off-putting.
3. **One channel = one point of failure** — The critiqued setter only used Facebook. No email, no phone, no other platform. When Otto said "message me on Discord," the setter refused. That refusal killed the deal. Expand channels — go wherever the prospect is.
4. **If they give you a positive signal, earn it** — A prospect who says "DM me on Discord" is showing buying intent. Creating a free Discord account takes 2 minutes. Not doing it signals you don't believe in your product enough to work for the sale.
5. **Never say "I'm not selling"** — You are selling. Every appointment setter is part of a sales process. Denying it when confronted destroys trust instantly. Own it. Selling is honourable.
6. **Stay positive even when they push back** — If a prospect says something inaccurate or aggressive, do NOT get defensive or passive-aggressive. Reflect abundance. Find a path forward. One neutral reply + one positive reply from the prospect = they're still warm.
7. **Meme/GIF follow-ups have a ceiling** — Effective for some audiences but turns off older, more experienced business owners. Know your niche. Contractors who've been in business 10+ years have seen everything. Don't over-rely on memes as the only follow-up tool.
8. **Your job is to make them believe you believe in your product** — Confidence and conviction are contagious. If you don't radiate certainty, they won't feel it. The prospect doesn't need to believe in you — they need to believe that YOU believe in you.
9. **Engagement is the Northstar, not the sale** — The worst outcome from cold outreach is no response. Any response — positive, negative, neutral — is progress. A "no" or pushback is infinitely more workable than silence.
10. **Refusing a prospect's terms is refusing the sale** — When a prospect tells you their terms (platform, time, format), accommodate them. They're doing you a favour by engaging at all. Work for it.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### What killed this setter's deal (live example):
```
WRONG: Used first name + generic pitch about "digital marketing clients"
       → Showed zero business understanding

WRONG: Only one channel (Facebook) — refused to go to Discord when asked
       → Shows low conviction in the product

WRONG: After neutral+positive responses, accused prospect of "not being interested"
       → Never assume non-interest from silence or ambiguity

WRONG: Said "my job is not to sell, I'm just an appointment setter"
       → Destroyed credibility; trust collapsed

WRONG: Passive-aggressive when prospect pushed back on his mindset
       → Any emotional charge from the setter = deal death

RIGHT WOULD HAVE BEEN: "You're right, I'll jump on Discord right now — give me 2 minutes."
```

## Frameworks

### Recovery Mindset — When Prospect Pushes Back
```
Prospect says something negative/inaccurate:
    ↓
Step 1: Do NOT correct them or get defensive
Step 2: Stay positive — "Totally fair, I get that"
Step 3: Find ANY opening to move forward
Step 4: Offer an alternative that accommodates their objection
Step 5: If they still shut down — disengage warmly, follow up later

Never: argue semantics / go passive-aggressive / assume they're "not interested"
```

### Channel Expansion Rule
```
They respond on Instagram → also find email + FB + phone
They say "contact me on X" → get on X immediately (even if new platform)
Never stay limited to one channel when signals are positive
Cross-channel reference: "Hey [name] — also sent this on [platform] in case it's easier"
```

## How to Apply — CortexaLabs

**Before sending the Trojan Horse:**
- Know the prospect's niche specifically (not just "you're a contractor")
- Know their city
- Know if they have a website (look it up)
- Know roughly how many Google reviews they have
- Know if their site is weak/non-existent (this is your specific angle)

**Adapted Trojan Horse for CortexaLabs:**
```
"Hey [name], thanks for following back. 
I looked up [business name] online — I work with [pool/roofing/landscaping] contractors specifically. 
I've got something I think could genuinely help, but I don't want to pitch anything without your permission. 
If you're open to hearing it, just reply with a thumbs up."
```

This version shows you actually looked them up — not just their name, but their business type. That one detail shifts the entire perception from "spam bot" to "person who did homework."

**If they ask you to contact them somewhere else:**
Immediately do it. Don't say "I don't use that platform." Open it, create a free account, message them. It takes 3 minutes and shows conviction.

**If they push back:**
Never defend. Never argue. Stay warm and find an alternative:
"That's totally fair — what would make it easier to talk about this?"

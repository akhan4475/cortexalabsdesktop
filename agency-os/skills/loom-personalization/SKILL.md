## Core Philosophy

The Loom is not a sales pitch. It is a trust accelerator.

Cold outbound starts at zero trust. High-ticket ($600–$1,100+ sites) requires trust to buy. Video is the richest medium you can use at scale — it lets the prospect see your face, hear your tone, and feel whether you're real. Text cannot do this.

**You are not trying to sell the website in the Loom. You are trying to build enough trust that they want to get on a call with you.**

The imperfect, genuine Loom outperforms the polished, scripted one every time.

---

## When to Send a Loom

**Always permission-based. Never cold Loom blast.**

Send a Loom when a lead:
- Has replied to your initial DM (any reply — positive, skeptical, or even a mild "what is this")
- Has agreed to watch a video ("yeah sure send it")
- Is at `dm_replied` stage and needs more than text to book a call

Do NOT send a Loom to:
- Cold prospects who haven't replied (waste — they won't watch it)
- Leads already at `call_booked` or beyond
- Leads with no website or Google listing to show on screen

The permission request: *"I pulled together a quick 2-min video showing exactly what I'd change about [business name]'s site. Want me to send it over?"*

**5–10 Looms per booked call** when permission-based. Watch rate approaches 80%+ when they asked for it.

## Personalization Data — Pull Before Recording

Do this in under 5 minutes:

- [ ] Open their Google Maps listing (get rating, review count, category)
- [ ] Open their current website (or note if they have none)
- [ ] Note 2-3 specific visual or structural problems with the site
- [ ] Find 1 competitor in their city with a better-looking site (for contrast)
- [ ] Check if they have Google reviews shown on their site (usually they don't)
- [ ] Note what niche + city they're in
- [ ] Find one specific, real detail about the business (a recent Instagram post, a service they offer that isn't on their site, an about-us detail) — this is what makes the hook land as personal, not templated
- [ ] If no website exists, pick the screen-share backdrop instead: their Instagram profile or Google Business listing works the same way — the backdrop itself doesn't have to be a website, it just has to be something real and specific to them on screen

## The Hook (First 20-40 Seconds — Where Most Looms Fail)

The single biggest failure mode in cold Looms is a weak opening — if it doesn't create curiosity fast, the prospect bounces before the pitch even starts. Three parts, in order:

1. **Face** — smile, small wave, say their name. The easy part, but skipping it makes the video feel cold immediately.
2. **Trigger/observation** — reference the one real, specific detail you found on their profile/site (see checklist above). This is what separates a personal video from a templated one.
3. **Light humor tied to the observation** (optional, use judgment) — makes you read as a real person. Don't force it if nothing natural comes to mind — a flat, genuine observation beats a forced joke.

```
"Hey [name] — [smile/wave]. Noticed [specific real detail about their business]. [optional light, natural comment]."
```

Then a transition line that sets up a payoff before you deliver it: *"Got a quick idea that could help with [outcome] — take a look."*

## Loom Video Structure (2–3 minutes max)

### [0:00 – 0:15] Open: Use the Hook (see above) Then Establish You're Real
Don't start with "Hi my name is Ayaan and I work at CortexaLabs." Lead with the Hook structure above (face → specific observation → transition line), then briefly establish who you are.

```
"Hey [first name] — [hook: specific observation + optional light comment]. I'm Ayaan, put together a quick idea for [business name] — take a look."
```

Face cam on. Do NOT read from a script — this kills the authenticity. Know the beats and talk naturally through them.

### [0:15 – 1:00] Their Current Situation — Screen Share Their Site
Pull up their website live. Don't criticize their business — critique the site structure.

What to show and say (pick 2-3 that apply):
- **No clear call-to-action on homepage**: "A roofing customer lands here and... there's no immediate way to call or request a quote"
- **No reviews on the site**: "You've got [X] Google reviews but none of them are on your site — that's the first thing a customer wants to see"
- **Not mobile-friendly**: "Most of your customers are finding you on their phone — here's what this looks like on mobile..."
- **No before/after work shown**: "Pool companies that show their actual work in photos get 3x more click-throughs"
- **No website at all**: "I searched [business name] in [city] and there's no site coming up — that's a gap your competitors don't have"

Keep your tone curious and helpful. Not "this is bad." More "here's what I noticed and why it matters."

### Opening Frame — Lead With Purpose, Not Aesthetics
Before pointing out anything wrong with their site, state the point of the whole thing in one line. Real example that worked: *"This isn't just meant to look good — it's meant to help you rank on Google and bring in organic leads, people already searching for [service] in [city]."* Say this before showing any gaps — it sets up everything that follows as being in service of that goal, not as criticism.

### Google Business Profile Gap — Call It Out, Offer to Fix It
If a lead has no Google Business Profile (check this during pre-recording research), name it directly and offer to help set it up: *"I noticed you don't have a Google Business Profile set up yet — I can help you get that going and start collecting reviews on there too."* Don't imply this is included free in every tier — CortexaLabs' GBP integration is part of Pro/Authority (`context/offer.md`); phrase it as something you'll help with, not a guarantee of scope, and let the actual offer get clarified on the call.

### Before/After Gallery — Frame Against the Buyer's Actual Fear
Don't just point at the feature. Name the objection it solves: *"A lot of people looking for [service] are doing it for the first time and want to see real examples before they commit — that's what this section is for."* This is stronger than "here's your project gallery" because it explains *why* it matters to the prospect's own customers, not just to them.

### [1:00 – 2:00] The Opportunity — Paint What Good Looks Like
Don't say "I'll fix all this for $X." Show them what the improved version looks like without pitching it.

Options:
- Pull up a competitor or CortexaLabs portfolio site in the same niche
- Describe what contractors with better sites are getting: "Pool contractors in [city] with a site that shows real project photos and has a click-to-call button are the ones getting called first on Google"
- Show a screenshot of a Google Maps result where the business with a good site dominates

Avoid: promising specific results, SEO rankings, or lead counts. Focus on the gap between them and their competition.

### [2:00 – 2:30] Social Proof (1 line, natural)
One line. Not a paragraph.

```
"I've built sites for [niche] contractors in [region] — [business name] in [city] got [outcome] within [timeframe]."
```

If no client yet: skip this section entirely. Don't fabricate. Move straight to CTA.

### Optional: Mistake → Solution → Outcome (for longer/more consultative Looms)
For prospects who need more convincing than a quick visual fix — name what contractors in their situation typically do instead of having a converting site, then tie it to the fix and a concrete result. Keep each line to one sentence; this is a rhythm, not a lecture.

```
"Most [niche] contractors either rely on word-of-mouth alone, or have a site that's just sort of... there. That usually means missed calls after hours, and no way to know if the site's even bringing anyone in.
A site built to convert fixes that — clear call button, reviews up front, so people book instead of bouncing.
That's the difference between a site that exists and one that's actually bringing in jobs."
```

Skip this section for shorter Looms — it's for cases where the prospect needs more context on *why* this matters, not for every video.

### [2:30 – 3:00] CTA — Lowest Friction Possible
Do NOT ask for a call or an in-depth reply as the first ask — that's too much commitment for a cold video. Pick the lowest-friction option that fits the moment, roughly in order of how easy it is for them to act on:

**1. Reaction (zero typing):**
```
"If this looks interesting, just react to this message with a 👍 and I'll follow up."
```

**2. One-word reply:**
```
"Happy to show you exactly what this could look like for [business name] — just reply 'yes' and I'll send over a couple times to grab 20 minutes."
```

**3. Invite a reply, not a booking link:**
```
"Let me know if you want to see what this looks like for [business name] and I'll jump on a quick call."
```

Do NOT send a Cal.com link in the Loom message itself — that's a separate step after they've replied (see "After Positive Reply to Loom" below).

**If price comes up before or during the Loom (e.g. in a DM reply first):** never quote a number in the video itself. Defer to the call: *"Pricing depends on the scope — easier to give you an accurate number once I know a bit more about your business. Worth a quick call?"* This isn't stonewalling — it's framed as needed for accuracy, and it's what actually moves the conversation to a call rather than ending it in DMs.

## Pre-Recording Checklist

- [ ] Close all tabs except prospect's site and competitor example
- [ ] Hide browser bookmarks bar
- [ ] Face cam on, good lighting (window or ring light)
- [ ] Quiet environment — no background noise
- [ ] Start recording ON their website, not on your home screen
- [ ] Know the 5 beats — no full script
- [ ] Total under 3 minutes
- [ ] Loom Chrome extension ready

## Post-Loom Nurture (If No Reply After 48h)

Loom sent and watched but no reply:
```
Hey [name] — shot you over that video. Curious what you thought — does any of that resonate with what you're seeing for [business name]?
```

Loom sent but NOT watched after 48h:
```
Hey [name] — don't think the video went through properly. Mind if I try again?
```

If they say yes: resend the Loom.

---

## After Positive Reply to Loom

Lead replies positively (says "yeah that's interesting" / "how much does it cost" / "when can we talk"):

**Immediately call them if you have their number.** If DM-only:
```
Great — let's grab a quick 20 minutes. Does [Thursday at 3pm] or [Friday at 11am] work better for you?
```

DO NOT send Cal.com link here. Propose specific times. Only send booking link after they choose a time.

Reconfirm evening before:
```
Hey [name] — just confirming we're still on for [time] tomorrow. Looking forward to it.
```

If no reconfirmation, follow up morning of:
```
Hey [name] — we're on for [time] today. Talk soon.
```

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Video is under 3 minutes
- [ ] Starts with their business on screen (not black screen, not your face first)
- [ ] Never says "AI", "automated", or "agency software"
- [ ] CTA is a reply invitation, not a "book here" link
- [ ] Face cam visible throughout
- [ ] Tone is genuine and curious, not rehearsed
- [ ] Recorded as Ayaan personally

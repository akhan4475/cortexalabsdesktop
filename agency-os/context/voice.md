# CortexaLabs AI — Voice & Tone Guide

## Core Tone Principle

Write like a person who builds things, not a SaaS founder pitching B2B software. Contractors are direct, no-nonsense, skeptical of slick marketing, and deeply attuned to authenticity. If your message sounds like a template, it gets ignored. If it sounds like a real person who looked at their specific business, it gets read.

The voice is: **direct, confident, specific, contractor-native.**

---

## Banned Phrases

These phrases trigger instant mental delete. Do not use any of them, in any context, in any DM, Loom, email, or follow-up. No exceptions.

1. "I came across your profile"
2. "I hope this message finds you well"
3. "I wanted to reach out"
4. "Just checking in"
5. "Touch base"
6. "Circle back"
7. "I'd love to connect"
8. "Leverage"
9. "Synergies"
10. "Game-changer"
11. "Revolutionary"
12. "Disruptive"
13. "Value-add"
14. "At the end of the day"
15. "Moving forward"
16. "Deep dive"
17. "Holistic approach"
18. "Reach out to discuss further"
19. "Don't hesitate to reach out"
20. "As per my last message"
21. "Circling back on this"
22. "Quick question"
23. "Hope you're doing well"
24. "I noticed you might be interested in"
25. "Looking forward to connecting"

**Why these kill deals:** Contractors are pitched constantly. They've seen every template. These phrases signal "mass DM from someone who doesn't know me." The moment they see one, they know it's automated and tune out.

## Good Openers

These work because they are specific, human, and about the prospect — not about us.

**Example 1 — Observation opener:**
"Saw you guys did a full pool build in Scottsdale recently — the deck work looked clean. Do you get most of your jobs from referrals?"

**Example 2 — Direct problem opener:**
"Looked up your site before messaging — it's not showing up when I search pool contractors in Phoenix. You're losing calls to guys who have worse reviews than you."

**Example 3 — Competitor angle:**
"Your competitor [Business Name] just got their site redone. Now they're showing up first in [city]. Figured you'd want to know."

**Example 4 — Compliment + gap:**
"Love the work you've got posted on Google. Whoever takes your photos knows what they're doing. Your website doesn't do it justice at all though."

**Example 5 — Pure direct:**
"I build websites for [niche] contractors in [city]. Saw yours and have a few ideas. Mind if I send a quick video?"

## DM Tone Rules

**Length:** Short. First DM should be 2–4 sentences max. You are not pitching — you are starting a conversation.

**Personalization required:** Every DM must reference at least one specific, real detail about the prospect's business. Business name alone does not count — it must be something you can only know by looking (a recent review, a service they list, a city they serve, a photo they have).

**No attachments on first DM:** Do not send the Loom link in the first message. Get a reply first ("mind if I send a quick video?"), then send the Loom.

Exception: If the opener is framed as "I made a quick video for you," you can include the Loom on the first DM. This works when the video is genuinely personalized (you recorded it specifically for them).

**No pitch on first DM:** The first message opens a conversation. It does not pitch a service, mention a price, or ask for a call. That comes after engagement.

**Reply tone:**
- Keep it short. Match their energy.
- If they reply with one line, reply with one or two lines.
- Do not over-explain.
- If they ask "how much?" — give the range directly. Don't evade.
- If they object — acknowledge it, don't argue. Use skills/objection-handling.md.

## Loom Video Tone

Loom videos are personalized screen recordings. They are the highest-converting part of our outreach. Tone rules:

**Conversational, not scripted:** It should sound like you're talking to them personally, not reading. Use their business name, their city, their specific services. If it could be sent to 10 other contractors, it's not personalized enough.

**Structure:**
1. Open by showing their current website or Google listing (0–10 seconds): "Hey [Name], pulled up your site real quick —"
2. Point out 2–3 specific issues or missed opportunities (10–40 seconds): Be honest. Don't be brutal. "The site looks dated, it's not mobile-friendly, and there's no easy way for someone to contact you after 5pm."
3. Show the comparison (40–60 seconds): Pull up a competitor or example of what we'd build. "Here's what your competitor looks like — they're probably getting the calls you're missing."
4. The ask (60–90 seconds max): "I put together a quick concept for what yours could look like. Want me to send it over?" — or — "We can have something like this live for you in about 10 days for $800. Worth a conversation?"

**Hard limits:**
- Never go over 90 seconds
- Never use a teleprompter (it shows)
- Never read from a script word-for-word
- Always say their business name at least twice
- Show their actual business at the start — don't just talk about it

## Follow-Up Tone

**Day 3 follow-up:** Casual. Not desperate. "Sent this over a few days ago — figured I'd bump it up in case it got buried." One sentence.

**Day 7 follow-up:** Slightly more direct. Mention what they're missing. "Still here if you want to talk. Pool season's almost halfway through — happy to get something live for you fast."

**Final follow-up (Day 14):** Close the loop. Give them an easy out or an easy yes. "Last follow-up — if the timing isn't right, totally get it. If you want to revisit this in the fall, just reply and I'll send over some options."

**What NOT to do in follow-ups:**
- Do not guilt-trip ("I've been waiting to hear from you")
- Do not be aggressive ("You're missing out" as the entire message)
- Do not apologize for following up
- Do not re-pitch the entire service in a follow-up
- Do not send more than 3 follow-ups total

## Call Tone

On sales calls, the voice shifts slightly — still direct and confident, but warmer and more curious.

- Open with a genuine question about their business, not a pitch
- Let them talk about their problems before you present solutions
- Do not interrupt or talk over them
- Match their vocabulary (say "jobs" not "projects," say "customers" not "clients," say "getting calls" not "lead generation")
- When they object, say "that's fair" first, then respond
- Never use passive voice to close: "So would you like to move forward?" — use active: "Let's get this started — I'll send the invoice now."
